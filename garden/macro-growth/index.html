I. Solow-Swan model

Supply-side economics and exogenous growth models

I.1 Setting

  • closed economy (no trade)
  • only producers and consumers, no government (i.e. saving by households flows directly into capital formation)
  • labor force is exogenous and grows at a constant growth rate $n=\dot{L_t}/L_t$
  • technology is exogenous and grows at the constant rate $g=\dot{A_t}/A_t$
  • capital accumulation (i.e. saving) is endogenous

Exogenous growth model: the long-term growth rate or remains constant at $g$, which is exogenous.

Exogenous variables are decided by the modeler. Endogenous variables, which are decided in the model, reflects the economic mechanisms we want to uncover through the model.

I.2 Hypothesis

  • saving rate $s\in(0,1)$ is exogenous and constant
  • all the production is the income of households
  • all the saving is made by the households
  • in every period, saving by households is always a fraction $s$ of income, $1-s$ for consumption

I.3 Production Function

$Y_t=F(K_t,A_tL_t)$ has constant returns to scale, and we rewrite it as: $$\frac{Y_t}{A_tL_t}=\frac{1}{A_tL_t}F(K_t,A_tL_t)=F(k_t,1)\equiv{f(k_t)}$$

$f(k_t)$ has following properties:

  • $f:\mathbb{R}^{+}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{+}$, $z\mapsto f(z)$, with $f(0)=0$
  • $f$ is strictly increasing: $\forall z \in \mathbb{R}^{+}$, $f\prime(z)>0$
  • $f$ is strictly concave: $\forall z \in \mathbb{R}^{+}$, $f\prime\prime(z)<0$
  • $f$ satisfies the Inada condition: $\lim_{z \to 0+} f\prime(z)=+\infty$, $\lim_{z \to +\infty} f\prime(z)=0$

I.4 Dynamics

The change in the capital stock is fresh saving net of depreciation, giving the key differential equation: $$\dot{K_t}=sY_t-\delta K_t$$

Then we can get the key equation of the Solow model: $$\dot{k_t}=sf(k_t)-(n+g+\delta)k_t$$

It states that the rate of change of the capital stock per unit of effective labor is the difference between two terms. The first, $sf(k_t)$, is actual investment per unit of effective labor. The second term, $(n + g +\delta)k_t$, is break-even investment, the amount of investment that must be done just to keep $k_t$ at its existing level.

I.5 Steady State and Balanced Growth Path

There is a unique level $k^{\ast}$ of capital to efficiency-labor such that, once it is attained, the economy remains in a steady state forever. The steady state is defined by $$\dot{k_t}=sf(k^{\ast})-(n+g+\delta)k^{\ast}=0$$

In the steady state, new capital accumulation just offsets the three dynamic forces ($n$, $g$, and $\delta$) reducing the ratio of capital to effective labor, so there is no tendency for capital deepening or dilution. Although consumption per effciency labor unit in steady state is constant, consumption per worker grows at the rate of technological advance, $g$. So does output per worker $Y_t/L_t$, the wage $w$, and capital per worker $K_t/L_t$. This is a path of balanced growth.

I.6 Implications of the Solow Model

I.6.1 What is the source of economic growth?

For the production growth rate per capita $G_t=\dot{y_t}/y_t$, with $y_t=A_tf(k_t)$, we have $G_t=\dot{A_t}/A_t+f\prime(k_t)\dot{k_t}/f(k_t)=g+f\prime(k_t)\dot{k_t}/f(k_t)$. With $\lim_{t \to +\infty}f\prime(k_t)\dot{k_t}/f(k_t) = 0$, we can get $$\lim_{t \to +\infty}G_t=g$$

Capital accumulation does not contribute to long-term economic growth. The long-term growth rate is equal to the technological advance rate. Without technological advance, the growth of production per capita will eventually stop.

I.6.2 What is an appropriate savings rate?

China is famous for its high saving rate. Now we can discuss under the Solow framework, at the steady state the output per capita

$$\begin{aligned} y_t&=Y_t/L_t\\ &=A_tL_tf(k^\ast)/L_t \\ &=A_tf(k^{\ast})\\ &=A_t(\frac{n+g+\delta}{s})k^{\ast} \end{aligned}$$

If we consider the optimal saving rate in terms of the welfare of households. The welfare is determined by their level of consumption: the higher the level of consumption, the higher the welfare.

The per capita consumption

$$\begin{aligned} c_t&=(1-s)y_t \\ &=(1-s) A_tf(k^{\ast})\\ &=A_tf(k^{\ast})-sA_tf(k^{\ast})\\ &=A_tf(k^{\ast})-A_t(n+g+\delta)k^{\ast}\\ &=A_t[f(k^{\ast})-(n+g+\delta)k^{\ast}] \end{aligned}$$ So we can get the $s_{gold}$ is the unique value such that

$$f^\prime(k^{\ast})=n+g+\delta$$

欲使每个人的消费达到最大，则对每个人的资本两的选择应使的边际产出等于劳动的增长率

I.7 Empirical Analysis of China’s Economic Growth

From 1965 to 2008, the contribution of technological progress to China’s GDP growth rate gradually increased. However, after the subprime crisis, its contribution fell sharply and became the main cause of the economic slowdown.

If it is interpreted from the perspective of production, it means that production technology has fallen sharply, or there has been a problem in resource allocation. At the same level of capital and labor input, the China’s economy cannot produce as many products as before. But such logic seems to be difficult to find evidence at the micro level.

Our next question is: What is in the black box of technology that is causing the slowdown in China’s economic growth?

II. From supply to demand

As we have seen in the first part, the Solow-Swan model only considers the supply-side, which is concerned with how to produce, and views the economy as an objective machine. However, we need to think of the economy as a complex system that combines the objective world with the subjective will.

II.1 Rational Expectations Revolution

Disappearance of the Phillips Curve in 1970s

We can think of the government and the people as two agents in the Stackelberg model. When the government starts to consciously keep unemployment down by pushing up inflation, the population will gradually realise that higher inflation comes from the government printing more money, not from the economy getting better, and therefore will not work more because of higher inflation. At this point inflation no longer has a dampening effect on unemployment, but only brings about a “stagflation” where high inflation and high unemployment go hand in hand.

Lucas critique argues that it is naive to try to predict the effects of a change in economic policy entirely on the basis of relationships observed in historical data, especially highly aggregated historical data. Macro analysis requires a micro foundation.When thinking about macro policy, it is important to anticipate the optimal response of the population to macro policy and to take this response into account when designing policy

II.2 General equilibrium

General equilibrium: all parts of the economy are in equilibrium at the same time.

Based on the Lucasian critique, the macro model must take the optimisation problem of the micro individual in the economy as the starting point, i.e. rationality.

Equilibrium means 2 points:

  • All micro individuals reach their optimum
  • All markets are cleared

II.3 Welfare

When we start thinking about “what should be”, economics is judged by welfare.

Welfare can be measured by utility.

Pareto efficiency

Pareto efficiency or Pareto optimality is a situation where no individual or preference criterion can be made better off without making at least one individual or preference criterion worse off.

Pareto optimality is a concept of efficiency. When an economy does not reach Pareto optimality, it means that resources are not being used to their full potential. Pareto optimality is the criterion by which economics thinks about “what should be”

Fundamental theorems of welfare economics

  1. The first states that in economic equilibrium, a set of complete markets, with complete information, and in perfect competition, will be Pareto optimal.

This means all competitive equilibria are Pareto optimal. In other words, competitive equilibria are “good” when measured by Pareto optimality.

  1. The second states that any Pareto optimum can be supported as a competitive equilibrium for some initial set of endowments.

This means competitive equilibrium can be used to achieve any efficient allocation.

Two theorems establish an equivalence (under certain conditions) between equilibrium and Pareto optimality.

III. Ramsey model

Determination of consumption and savings

  • supply-side: enterprises
  • demand-side: households

Two endogenous variables:

  1. control variable
  2. state variable

III.1 Households

Setup

Each household wishes to maximize overall utility $U$, given by

$$U_0=\int_{0}^{+\infty} e^{-\rho t} \cdot L_t \cdot u(c_t)dt=L_0 \int_{0}^{+\infty} e^{-(\rho-n) t} \cdot u(c_t)dt$$

where

  • $c_t=\frac{C_t}{L_t}$ per capita consumption
  • $\rho$ the rate of time preference. $\rho >0$ means that utilities are valued less the later they are received.
  • $u(\cdot)$ instantaneous utility (increasing, concave, Inada)

budget constraint

  • $B_t$ : total asset
  • $b_t=\frac{B_t}{L_t}$ : asset per capita
  • $r_t$: interest rate

$$\dot{B_t}=r_tB_t+w_tL_t-C_t$$ $$\dot{b_t}=(r_t-n)b_t+w_t-c_t$$

Intertemporal budget constraint

$$\int_{0}^{+\infty}c_te^{{-\int_{0}^t(r_\tau-n)d\tau}}dt \leq b_0+\int_{0}^{+\infty}w_te^{{-\int_{0}^t(r_\tau-n)d\tau}}dt$$

non-Ponzi condition

$$\lim_{T \to \infty}[b_T e^{{-\int_{0}^t(r_\tau-n)d\tau}}dt] \geq 0$$

Household’s mission: maximise utility

$$\max_{c_t, b_t} [L_0 \int_{0}^{+\infty} e^{-(\rho-n) t} \cdot u(c_t)dt]$$

s.c.

  • $\forall t, c_t\geq0$
  • $\forall t, \dot{b_t}=(r_t-n)b_t+w_t-c_t$
  • $\lim_{T \to \infty}[b_T e^{{-\int_{0}^t(r_\tau-n)d\tau}}dt] \geq 0$

Hamiltonian

  • $b_t$ : state variable - value determined in period $t-1$
  • $c_t$ : control variable - value determined in period $t$
  • $\lambda_t$ co-variable

$$H(c_t,b_t,\lambda_t,t)= e^{-(\rho-n) t} u(c_t)+\lambda_t[(r_t-n)b_t+w_t-c_t]$$

  • $\forall t\geq 0, \frac{\partial{H}}{\partial{c_t}}=0$
  • $\forall t\geq 0, \frac{\partial{H}}{\partial{b_t}}=-\dot{\lambda_t}$
  • $\forall t\geq 0, \dot{b_t}=(r_t-n)b_t+w_t-c_t$
  • $\lim_{t \to \infty} b_t \lambda_t = 0$
  • $\lim_{t \to \infty}[b_t e^{{-\int_{0}^t(r_\tau-n)d\tau}}dt] \geq 0$
  • $\forall t \geq 0, c_t \geq 0, \lambda_t \geq 0$

Euler equation

$$\frac{u^\prime(c_t)}{u(c_t)}=\rho-r_t$$

$$\frac{\dot{c_t}}{c_t}=[\frac{-u^{\prime \prime}(c_t)\dot{c_t}}{u^\prime(c_t)}]^{-1}(r_t-\rho)$$

elasticity of intertemporal substitution

$$\sigma(c_t)=[\frac{-u^{\prime \prime}(c_t)c_t}{u^\prime(c_t)}]^{-1}$$
